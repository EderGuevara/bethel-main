CHAPTER 8
Group Life Insurance
Master how group life insurance works — master policies, certificates of insurance, contributory vs. non-contributory plans, conversion privileges, and the $50,000 tax rule.
Accidental Death & Dismemberment (AD&D)
Benefit paid in addition to group life; Principal Sum for accidental death; Capital Sum (% of Principal Sum) for accidental loss of limb or sight
Principal Sum
The full face amount paid under AD&D coverage if death results from a covered accident
Capital Sum
Percentage of the Principal Sum paid for accidental dismemberment; typically 50% for one limb or eye, 100% for two or more
Actively at Work Provision
Requires an employee to be actively working on the effective date of group coverage; coverage is delayed if the employee is absent due to illness or injury
Portability
Right of a departing employee to continue group TERM life coverage at their own expense after leaving the employer group
Probationary Period
Waiting period (typically 30–90 days from hire) a new employee must satisfy before becoming eligible to enroll in group coverage
Key Terms
Master Policy
The contract issued to the employer (group policyholder). Contains all terms and benefits. Employees receive certificates, not the master policy.
Certificate of Insurance
Document given to each covered employee summarizing their benefits. NOT the actual policy — just evidence of coverage.
Contributory Plan
Both the employer and employees share the premium cost. At least 75% of eligible employees must participate.
Non-Contributory Plan
Employer pays 100% of the premium. ALL eligible employees are automatically enrolled. 100% participation required.
Conversion Privilege
When leaving the group (job loss, retirement, etc.), a covered employee can convert their group coverage to an individual permanent policy WITHOUT evidence of insurability.
Creditor Group Life
Group insurance purchased by a creditor on the lives of debtors. Benefit equal to outstanding loan balance.
COBRA
Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act — allows employees to continue group health coverage (NOT group life) after leaving employment for up to 18 months, at their own expense.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
How Group Underwriting Works
Group insurance is underwritten differently from individual insurance:
  • The group is underwritten — not individuals. Individual members generally do NOT need a medical exam.
  • Adverse selection is controlled by requiring a minimum group size and minimum participation percentages.
  • The insurer evaluates the group's age distribution, occupation, industry, and claims history.
  • New employees joining the group during an open enrollment period are accepted without medical questions.
  • Late enrollees (outside open enrollment) may need to provide evidence of insurability.
Core Concept
Eligible Groups for Group Insurance
Not just any group qualifies. To be eligible, the group must:
  • Have been formed for a purpose other than obtaining insurance
  • Have a minimum number of members (often 10, varies by state and carrier)
  • Have a flow of new members to keep the group healthy (e.g., employee turnover)
Eligible groups include: employer-employee groups, labor unions, association groups (professional/trade), creditor groups, and fraternal organizations.
Core Concept
The Conversion Privilege
This is one of the most important group life features on the exam. When an employee leaves the group (for any reason), they have the right to:
  • Convert group term coverage to an individual permanent policy (whole life, NOT term)
  • Do so without evidence of insurability (no medical exam)
  • Apply within a set window — typically 31 days after leaving the group
  • Pay premiums at their own expense at the rate for their current age and class of risk
The converted policy is generally at a higher premium since it's now individual permanent insurance.

Exam Tip
Conversion Is to Permanent — Not Term
When an employee exercises the conversion privilege after leaving a group, the coverage converts to an individual permanent policy (typically whole life), not another term policy. This is a frequent exam trap. The exam may try to get you to say they can convert to "whatever they want" — the answer is permanent insurance only.
Exam Tip
COBRA Does NOT Cover Group Life
COBRA is a federal law that allows continued coverage for group health insurance only (medical, dental, vision). It does NOT apply to group life insurance. When a person leaves a group, they can use the conversion privilege for life — but there is no COBRA equivalent for group life.
Watch Out
$50,000 Group Life Tax Rule
Employer-paid group term life insurance is a tax-free benefit — up to $50,000. Coverage above that amount creates taxable income for the employee. The IRS uses "Table I" rates to calculate the imputed income. The employee must include this in their W-2. This is a highly tested number — know it cold.
Group AD&D — Accidental Death & Dismemberment
Core Concept
How Group AD&D Works
  • Paid in addition to the group life death benefit
  • Principal Sum: paid if death results from a covered accident
  • Capital Sum: paid for accidental dismemberment or loss of sight/hearing
  • Loss of ONE hand, foot, or eye = 50% of Principal Sum
  • Loss of TWO or more limbs or eyes = 100% of Principal Sum
  • Common exclusions: illness, self-inflicted injury, war, intoxication
Key Numbers
AD&D Capital Sum Schedule
100%Capital Sum for loss of TWO limbs or eyes (= Principal Sum)
50%Capital Sum for loss of ONE limb or eye
Watch Out
AD&D Exclusions — Accidental Only
AD&D pays only for accidental death or dismemberment. Death from illness, suicide, or intoxication is NOT covered. Read every scenario carefully — "accidental" must be the cause of death or injury.
Group Life Eligibility & Continuation
Core Concept
Actively at Work Provision
An employee must be actively at work on the effective date of coverage. If the employee is absent due to illness or disability on the effective date, group coverage is delayed until the employee returns to active work. This prevents a seriously ill employee from being added to the group plan on the coverage effective date.
Core Concept
Probationary Period
A waiting period — typically 30, 60, or 90 days from the date of hire — before a new employee is eligible to enroll in the group plan. Purpose: reduce administrative burden and adverse selection. During the probationary period, the employee has NO coverage under the group life plan.
Core Concept
Dependents Coverage
  • Group life plans may extend coverage to the employee's dependents
  • Spouse: typically a percentage of the employee's face amount
  • Children: covered from live birth to age 26 per ACA rules
  • Dependent coverage is usually optional (employee-paid)
  • Dependent face amounts are typically small (e.g., $5,000–$10,000)
  • Evidence of insurability generally NOT required during open enrollment
Core Concept
Portability vs. Conversion
  • Conversion: departing employee converts group TERM to an individual PERMANENT policy (whole life); no new underwriting; higher premium; 31-day conversion period
  • Portability: departing employee keeps group TERM coverage and continues paying premiums; retains term insurance rather than converting to permanent
  • Key difference: Conversion = permanent policy. Portability = continued term coverage.
Exam Tip
Portability vs. Conversion
"What option allows a terminated employee to keep term life coverage?" → Portability. "What converts group coverage to a cash-value policy?" → Conversion. Conversion always produces a permanent policy. Portability keeps it as term.
Portability & Dependents Coverage — Key Rules
Exam Tip
Group Life Portability
Allows an employee to take their group TERM coverage with them when they leave the group — WITHOUT converting to permanent insurance. Unlike conversion (which requires switching to whole life at attained-age rates), portability keeps the term insurance in force for a limited period. KEY RULE: Conversion = permanent (whole life). Portability = stays as term. Both allow departing employees to keep coverage.
Exam Tip
Dependents Coverage Under Group Plans
Spouses and dependent children may be covered under a group life plan. Dependent children are typically covered to age 26 (ACA requirement). Face amounts for dependents are usually lower than employee coverage (e.g., $5,000–$10,000). When an employee leaves the group, the dependent's coverage can also be converted to individual permanent coverage. KEY RULE: Children covered to age 26 under group plans (ACA). Dependents have conversion rights when employee leaves.
