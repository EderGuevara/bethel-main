CHAPTER 9
Annuities
Understand annuities as the opposite of life insurance — they protect against outliving your money. Learn accumulation vs. annuitization phases, fixed/variable/indexed types, payout options, and tax treatment.
Market Value Adjustment (MVA)
Adjustment applied to fixed annuity surrenders reflecting the change in interest rates since purchase; rates up = negative MVA; rates down = positive MVA
Nursing Home Waiver
Provision allowing annuity surrender without surrender charges if the owner is confined to a qualified nursing home for a specified period (typically 30–90+ consecutive days)
Refund Annuity (Installment Refund)
Payout option guaranteeing that if the annuitant dies before recovering total premiums paid, the remainder is paid to the beneficiary in installments
Free Withdrawal Provision
Contract right to withdraw up to a specified percentage (typically 10%) of annuity value each year without incurring surrender charges
Key Terms
Annuity
A contract where the annuitant pays premiums (lump sum or over time) and the insurer guarantees periodic income payments — protecting against longevity risk.
Accumulation Phase
The period when money grows inside the annuity. Tax-deferred growth. No taxes until money is withdrawn.
Annuitization Phase
The payout phase — once triggered, the annuitant receives periodic income payments. Once started, cannot be reversed.
Immediate Annuity
Funded with a lump sum; income payments begin within one month (or one payment period). Purchased by those already in retirement.
Deferred Annuity
Income payments begin at a future date. Can be funded with a lump sum or over time. Most common type sold.
Fixed Annuity
Insurer guarantees a minimum interest rate on the accumulation. Risk is on the insurer. Predictable and safe.
Variable Annuity
Funds invested in separate accounts (like mutual funds). Returns vary; risk is on the annuitant. Requires a securities license.
Indexed Annuity (FIA)
Returns tied to a market index (e.g., S&P 500) with a floor (no loss) and a cap on upside. Hybrid of fixed and variable.
Surrender Charge
Fee for withdrawing more than allowed during the surrender period (typically 5–10 years). Decreases over time.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Annuity vs. Life Insurance — The Contrast
Life insurance and annuities are opposite products from the same companies:
  • Life insurance — protects against dying too soon (financial loss to family)
  • Annuity — protects against living too long (outliving your money — longevity risk)
Both offer tax-deferred growth. The annuity is funded by the annuitant; the life policy is funded to protect others. Annuities have NO death benefit in the traditional sense unless a rider is added.
Core Concept
Annuity Payout Options
Once annuitized, the annuitant selects a payout option. These mirror life insurance settlement options:
  • Life only (straight life) — highest monthly payment; payments stop at annuitant's death (risk: die early, insurer keeps balance)
  • Life with period certain — payments for life, but guaranteed for a minimum period (e.g., 10 or 20 years). If annuitant dies early, remaining payments go to beneficiary.
  • Joint and survivor — covers two people; payments continue as long as either is alive (common for spouses)
  • Fixed period — payments over a set number of years, regardless of when annuitant dies
Core Concept
Tax Treatment of Annuities — LIFO Rule
Annuities are funded with after-tax dollars (non-qualified) or pre-tax dollars (qualified, e.g., in an IRA):
  • Non-qualified annuity withdrawals are taxed LIFO — Last In, First Out. This means earnings come out first and are taxable before the cost basis (principal) is returned.
  • Earnings are taxable as ordinary income when withdrawn
  • 10% early withdrawal penalty applies before age 59½
  • Once annuitized, each payment is part return of principal (not taxed) and part earnings (taxed) — called the exclusion ratio

Exam Tip
Life Only Annuity — Highest Payment, Highest Risk
The life only (straight life) annuity provides the highest monthly payment because it pays for life with no guaranteed period — if the annuitant dies the day after starting payments, the insurer keeps everything. It's the most common exam comparison: life only = highest payment but beneficiary gets nothing if annuitant dies early.
Exam Tip
Variable Annuity Requires a Securities License
Like variable life insurance, a variable annuity requires both a state insurance license AND a federal securities registration (FINRA Series 6 or 7). Fixed and fixed-indexed annuities require only an insurance license. This distinction is frequently tested.
Watch Out
Annuity Taxation vs. Life Insurance Taxation
Life insurance death benefits are generally income-tax free. Annuity distributions are generally taxable as ordinary income (the earnings portion). This is a key difference. Also: annuity withdrawals before 59½ are hit with a 10% penalty. Life insurance policy loans have no such penalty as long as the policy stays in force.
Market Value Adjustment (MVA)
Core Concept
How MVA Works
  • Applies when a fixed annuity is surrendered before the end of the guarantee period
  • If interest rates have RISEN since purchase: MVA is negative (reduces surrender value)
  • If interest rates have FALLEN since purchase: MVA is positive (increases surrender value)
  • Purpose: protects the insurer from losses when market rates change
  • MVA is applied in addition to any surrender charge
Exam Tip
Remember the MVA Direction
Rate UP → MVA hurts the annuitant (negative). Rate DOWN → MVA helps the annuitant (positive). Think of it like a bond: when rates rise, the value of existing fixed-rate contracts falls — so early surrender produces less money.
Annuity Surrender Provisions
Core Concept
Surrender Charges & Free Withdrawal
  • Surrender charge period: typically 5–10 years; charges decline each year (e.g., 7%, 6%, 5%, 4%...)
  • Free withdrawal provision: usually 10% of account value per year can be withdrawn penalty-free
  • After the surrender period ends: no surrender charge applies
  • Surrender charges are separate from the IRS 10% early distribution penalty
Core Concept
Nursing Home Waiver
Most annuity contracts include a waiver of surrender charges if the owner is confined to a qualified nursing home or LTC facility for a specified period (typically 30, 60, or 90+ consecutive days). Provides liquidity in a medical emergency without incurring surrender penalties. Specific terms vary by contract — always check the policy. Does NOT waive the IRS early distribution penalty (if applicable).
Core Concept
Refund Annuity (Installment Refund)
Payout option that guarantees the company will pay at least the total premiums paid into the contract. If the annuitant dies before receiving the full amount paid in, the remainder is paid to the beneficiary in installments (not a lump sum — that would be a Cash Refund annuity). Provides a safety net but produces lower monthly payments than Life Only.
Exam Tip
Payout Options Compared
  • Life Only: highest payment per month; nothing to beneficiary at death
  • Life with Period Certain: payments guaranteed for minimum period (10 or 20 years) even if annuitant dies early
  • Joint & Survivor: payments continue to surviving annuitant (usually reduced)
  • Installment Refund: beneficiary gets remaining premiums in installments
  • Cash Refund: beneficiary gets remaining premiums as a lump sum
Parties to an Annuity & Nursing Home Waiver
Core Concept
Parties to an Annuity Contract
  • Owner: pays premiums and controls the contract — can be same as annuitant or different
  • Annuitant: the MEASURING LIFE — whose lifespan determines income payments. Once annuitized, payments are based on the annuitant's life.
  • Beneficiary: receives any remaining value or guaranteed payments if the annuitant dies before they are exhausted
KEY RULE: Owner controls. Annuitant measures the payments. Beneficiary inherits remaining value. These are THREE separate roles that CAN be held by the same person or different people.
Core Concept
Nursing Home (Confinement) Waiver
A rider on deferred annuities that waives surrender charges if the owner is confined to a nursing home (or qualified care facility) for a minimum consecutive period (typically 30, 60, or 90 days). Allows the owner to access their annuity funds without penalty during a long-term care event. KEY RULE: Nursing home waiver = surrender charges waived if owner requires nursing home care for the specified minimum period.
Quick Recall — Must Know Before Moving On
Annuity = protects against outliving money (longevity risk). Life insurance = protects against dying too soon.
Accumulation phase = money grows tax-deferred. Annuitization phase = periodic income payments begin.
Immediate annuity = lump sum, income starts within 1 month. Deferred = future income start date.
Fixed = insurer bears risk (guaranteed rate). Variable = annuitant bears risk (separate accounts).
Variable annuity = requires insurance license + securities license (Series 6 or 7).
LIFO tax rule: earnings come out first on non-qualified withdrawals — taxed as ordinary income.
10% penalty for withdrawals before age 59½ (plus ordinary income tax).
Life only payout = highest monthly payment; stops at death with nothing to beneficiary.
1035 exchange: annuity → annuity is tax-free. Annuity → life insurance is NOT allowed tax-free.
MVA: rates rise → negative adjustment on surrender. Rates fall → positive adjustment. Applied in addition to surrender charge.
Free withdrawal: typically 10% of account value per year without surrender charge.
Nursing home waiver: no surrender charge after 30–90+ consecutive days of LTC confinement.
Installment refund: beneficiary gets remaining premiums in installments if annuitant dies early. Cash refund = lump sum.
