CHAPTER 3
Legal Concepts of the Insurance Contract
Master the legal requirements for a valid contract, the unique characteristics of insurance contracts, and the doctrines of good faith that govern both parties.
Express Authority
Authority specifically granted to an agent in the written agency contract
Implied Authority
Authority NOT written in the agent contract but reasonably necessary to carry out express authority (e.g., explaining policy terms)
Apparent Authority
Authority the public reasonably believes the agent has based on the principal's (insurer's) actions or representations, even if not actually granted
Key Terms
Offer & Acceptance
Applicant makes the offer (submits application + premium). Insurer accepts by issuing the policy.
Consideration
Something of value exchanged. Applicant's consideration = premium + application. Insurer's = promise to pay claims.
Legal Purpose
The contract must be for a lawful purpose. Insuring criminal activity is void.
Competent Parties
Both parties must be of legal age and sound mind. Minors and mentally incompetent persons cannot form a valid contract.
Aleatory Contract
Unequal exchange of value — the insured may pay little in premiums but receive a large death benefit, or pay for years and never collect.
Unilateral Contract
Only ONE party (the insurer) makes a legally enforceable promise. The insured cannot be forced to pay premiums.
Contract of Adhesion
Policy is drafted entirely by the insurer; insured can only accept or reject. Ambiguities are interpreted in favor of the insured.
Conditional Contract
The insurer's obligation to pay is conditional on the insured meeting policy requirements (paying premiums, filing timely claims).
Representation
A statement believed to be true at the time made. Does NOT have to be literally true — just substantially true.
Warranty
A statement guaranteed to be absolutely true. Breach of warranty — even innocent — can void the policy.
Concealment
Intentional withholding of material information from the insurer. Grounds for voiding the policy.
Waiver & Estoppel
Waiver = insurer voluntarily gives up a known right. Estoppel = insurer is prevented from denying coverage due to its own prior conduct.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
COAL — The 4 Requirements for a Valid Contract
Every valid contract — including insurance — requires all four elements. Memory trick: COAL
  • Consideration — something of value from both parties
  • Offer & Acceptance — a clear offer and unconditional acceptance
  • Agree to legal purpose — must be for a lawful objective
  • Legal competence — both parties must be legally capable
If any one element is missing, the contract is void or voidable.
Core Concept
4 Unique Characteristics of Insurance Contracts
Insurance contracts are different from regular contracts in four important ways:
  • Aleatory — unequal exchange (you may collect far more than you paid, or nothing)
  • Unilateral — only the insurer makes a binding promise; insured can stop paying at any time
  • Adhesion — take-it-or-leave-it; insurer writes it; ambiguities favor the insured
  • Conditional — insurer only pays if conditions are met (premium paid, timely claim, etc.)
Core Concept
Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)
Insurance contracts require a higher standard of honesty than regular commercial contracts. Both parties must act in complete good faith. The insured must disclose all material facts; the insurer must deal fairly with claims. Violations include:
  • Misrepresentation — false statement of a material fact
  • Concealment — intentional withholding of material information
  • Fraud — intentional deception to obtain insurance or a claim payout

Exam Tip
Who Makes the Offer in an Insurance Contract?
The applicant makes the offer — not the insurer. When you submit an application (especially with the first premium), you are making an offer to the insurer. The insurer accepts by issuing the policy. This is a classic exam trick — most people assume the insurer offers and the applicant accepts.
Exam Tip
Adhesion = Ambiguity Favors the Insured
Because the insured had no say in drafting the policy (it's adhesion), any unclear or ambiguous language in the policy will be interpreted by courts in favor of the insured and against the insurer who wrote it. This is a key legal principle tested frequently.
Watch Out
Representations vs. Warranties — Know the Difference
Most statements on an insurance application are representations (believed to be true, not guaranteed). True warranties are rare in life insurance. An innocent misrepresentation on an application is grounds to void the policy only if it is material — meaning it would have affected the insurer's underwriting decision.
Agent Authority — Types (High Exam Priority)
Core Concept
Three Types of Agent Authority
  • Express Authority: spelled out in writing in the agency contract. Example: authority to solicit applications and collect initial premiums.
  • Implied Authority: not written, but flows naturally from express authority. Example: agent authorized to sell policies is impliedly authorized to explain and interpret policy terms to clients.
  • Apparent Authority: authority the public reasonably believes the agent has, created by the principal's (insurer's) conduct — even if the agent was not actually authorized to act that way.
Exam Tip
Apparent Authority — Insurer Is Bound
If a company provides an agent with business cards, company letterhead, and application forms, and the agent makes a promise or representation the company didn't authorize, the company may still be bound by that promise under apparent authority. The insurer created the appearance of authority — even if the agent exceeded it internally. The exam will describe a scenario: look for situations where the company's conduct led the public to believe the agent had authority.
Watch Out
Apparent Authority Has Limits
  • Apparent authority cannot override an act that is expressly prohibited in the agent contract AND clearly communicated to the public
  • An insurer can limit apparent authority by clearly notifying customers of the agent's restrictions
  • The hierarchy: Express > Implied > Apparent
