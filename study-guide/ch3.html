CHAPTER 3
Legal Concepts of the Insurance Contract
Understand the policy provisions that govern every life insurance contract, the types of agent authority, and the key legal rules tested most heavily on the licensing exam.
Key Terms — Agent Authority
Express Authority
Authority specifically granted to an agent in the written agency contract.
Implied Authority
Authority NOT written in the agent contract but reasonably necessary to carry out express authority (e.g., explaining policy terms).
Apparent Authority
Authority the public reasonably believes the agent has based on the principal's (insurer's) actions, even if not actually granted.
Key Terms — Policy Provisions
Entire Contract Clause
The policy plus the application constitute the complete agreement. The insurer cannot use outside statements to change or deny coverage.
Free-Look Period
A 10-day window (some states 20 days for seniors) after policy delivery to return the policy for a full premium refund, no questions asked.
Grace Period
A 30-day period (31 days for whole life) after a missed premium due date during which the policy remains in force.
Incontestability Clause
After the policy has been in force for 2 years, the insurer cannot contest it or void it for misrepresentation on the application.
Suicide Clause
If the insured commits suicide within the first 2 years of the policy, the insurer returns only the premiums paid — no death benefit.
Reinstatement
A lapsed policy may be reinstated within 3–5 years by providing proof of insurability and paying all overdue premiums plus interest.
Absolute Assignment
The policyholder permanently transfers ALL ownership rights in the policy to another party.
Collateral Assignment
The policyholder temporarily transfers limited policy rights as security for a loan. Rights revert to the owner once the loan is repaid.
Binding Receipt
Provides immediate coverage when the application and initial premium are submitted — regardless of insurability. Coverage begins at application date.
Conditional Receipt
Coverage is effective only if the applicant is found to be insurable as of the date of the application or medical exam (whichever is later).
Agent Authority — Types (High Exam Priority)
Core Concept
Three Types of Agent Authority
  • Express Authority: spelled out in writing in the agency contract. Example: authority to solicit applications and collect initial premiums.
  • Implied Authority: not written, but flows naturally from express authority. Example: agent authorized to sell policies is impliedly authorized to explain and interpret policy terms to clients.
  • Apparent Authority: authority the public reasonably believes the agent has, created by the principal's (insurer's) conduct — even if the agent was not actually authorized to act that way.
Exam Tip
Apparent Authority — Insurer Is Bound
If a company provides an agent with business cards, company letterhead, and application forms, and the agent makes a promise or representation the company didn't authorize, the company may still be bound by that promise under apparent authority. The insurer created the appearance of authority — even if the agent exceeded it internally. The exam will describe a scenario: look for situations where the company's conduct led the public to believe the agent had authority.
Watch Out
Apparent Authority Has Limits
  • Apparent authority cannot override an act that is expressly prohibited in the agent contract AND clearly communicated to the public
  • An insurer can limit apparent authority by clearly notifying customers of the agent's restrictions
  • The hierarchy: Express > Implied > Apparent
Policy Provisions — Critical Exam Topics
Core Concept
Entire Contract Clause
The policy and the attached application together form the entire contract between the insurer and the policyholder. No other documents, statements, or conversations are part of the contract. This protects the insured — the insurer cannot introduce outside evidence to change or deny coverage. It also means the insured is bound by everything in the application.
Core Concept
Free-Look Period & Grace Period
  • Free-Look Period (10 days): After the policy is delivered, the policyholder has 10 days to review it and return it for a full premium refund. Some states allow 20 days for senior applicants.
  • Grace Period (30 days): If a premium payment is missed, the policy stays in force for 30 days (31 days for whole life). If the insured dies during the grace period, the insurer pays the death benefit minus the overdue premium.
Core Concept
Incontestability Clause
After a life insurance policy has been in force for 2 years, the insurer can no longer contest or void it based on misrepresentation on the application — even if the insured lied. The 2-year period is meant to protect beneficiaries who had nothing to do with the application. Exception: fraud may still allow contestability in some states even after 2 years.
Core Concept
Suicide Clause
Most life insurance policies include a 2-year suicide exclusion. If the insured dies by suicide within the first 2 years of the policy, the insurer will not pay the full death benefit — it will only return the premiums paid. After 2 years, suicide is covered the same as any other cause of death.
Core Concept
Reinstatement
A lapsed policy can be reinstated (restored to active status) if the policyholder:
  • Applies for reinstatement within 3–5 years of the lapse
  • Provides satisfactory proof of insurability (evidence of good health)
  • Pays all back premiums plus interest
Reinstating a policy restores the original policy — including the original incontestability and suicide clause periods, which begin again from the reinstatement date.
Core Concept
Assignment — Absolute vs. Collateral
  • Absolute Assignment: The policyholder permanently and irrevocably transfers ALL ownership rights (right to name beneficiary, borrow, surrender, etc.) to another person or entity. Commonly used when donating a policy to a charity or as part of an estate plan.
  • Collateral Assignment: The policyholder temporarily transfers limited rights to a lender as security for a loan. The lender only gets rights up to the amount of the loan. Once the loan is repaid, full ownership rights revert back to the policyholder.
Core Concept
Binding Receipt vs. Conditional Receipt
Receipts are given to applicants when the initial premium is collected with the application:
  • Binding Receipt: Coverage begins immediately on the application date, regardless of whether the applicant would qualify medically. Rare — very favorable to the insured.
  • Conditional Receipt: Coverage only takes effect if and when the applicant is found to be insurable as of the date of the application or the date of the medical exam. Most common type of receipt used.

Exam Tip
Incontestability Does NOT Protect Fraud in All States
While the incontestability clause generally prevents the insurer from voiding a policy after 2 years, some states allow the insurer to contest a policy for intentional fraud even after 2 years have passed. If the exam says "misrepresentation," the 2-year rule applies. If the exam says "intentional fraud" or "fraudulent misrepresentation," check whether the state allows an exception — the question will usually tell you.
Exam Tip
Reinstatement Restarts Suicide & Incontestability Periods
When a lapsed policy is reinstated, the suicide clause and incontestability periods begin again from the reinstatement date — not from the original issue date. This is a heavily tested rule. The insurer takes on new risk with reinstatement, so it gets a fresh 2-year window on both clauses.
Watch Out
Conditional Receipt — Coverage Is NOT Guaranteed
Many students assume any receipt = immediate coverage. With a conditional receipt, if the applicant is found to be uninsurable (medically declined) after submitting the application, there is no coverage — even if the applicant dies between application and underwriting. The condition was not met. Only a binding receipt guarantees coverage from day one.
