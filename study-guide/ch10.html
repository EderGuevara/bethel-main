CHAPTER 10
Social Security & Medicare
Learn the Social Security program — FICA taxes, eligibility, retirement and disability benefits, survivors benefits — and the four parts of Medicare. These concepts directly tie to why life insurance and annuities are needed.
Blackout Period
Gap in SS survivor benefits; begins when youngest child turns 16, ends when surviving spouse reaches age 60; life insurance fills this income gap
Earnings Test
Pre-FRA rule: $1 withheld for every $2 earned above the annual limit; eliminated at Full Retirement Age
Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)
Reduces SS retirement benefit for workers who also receive a pension from employment NOT covered by Social Security; does not eliminate the benefit entirely
Combined Income
AGI + nontaxable interest + 50% of SS benefits; determines what percentage of SS benefits is subject to federal income tax
Key Terms
FICA
Federal Insurance Contributions Act. The payroll tax that funds Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%). Total: 7.65% each for employee and employer.
Fully Insured
Worker has earned 40 quarters of coverage (10 years of work). Required for full retirement benefits and most Social Security programs.
Currently Insured
Worker has earned at least 6 quarters of coverage in the last 13 quarters. Qualifies for limited survivors and disability benefits only.
Quarter of Coverage (Credit)
One credit earned for a set amount of earnings. Maximum 4 credits per year. 40 total credits = fully insured.
Full Retirement Age (FRA)
Age at which full Social Security retirement benefits are payable. Currently 66–67 depending on birth year.
Early Retirement
Social Security retirement benefits can begin as early as age 62 — but at a permanently reduced amount (up to 30% reduction).
Delayed Retirement
Delaying benefits past FRA increases monthly benefit by 8% per year (up to age 70).
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Social Security Benefits — 4 Types
Social Security provides four main categories of benefits:
  • Retirement benefits — for fully insured workers (40 credits). Begin at 62 (reduced) or FRA (full). Increased for each year delayed past FRA up to age 70.
  • Disability benefits (SSDI) — for fully insured workers (with additional recent-work requirement). Must be unable to perform any substantial work for at least 12 months. 5-month waiting period before benefits start.
  • Survivors benefits — paid to the spouse and children of a deceased fully or currently insured worker. A lump-sum death benefit of $255 is also paid to the surviving spouse or children.
  • Medicare — health insurance program for those age 65+ and certain disabled individuals.
Core Concept
Medicare — 4 Parts
Medicare has four distinct parts:
  • Part A (Hospital Insurance) — covers inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing, hospice. Funded by FICA taxes — no monthly premium for most people (if they have 40 work credits).
  • Part B (Medical Insurance) — covers outpatient doctor visits, lab tests, preventive care. Requires a monthly premium. Voluntary enrollment.
  • Part C (Medicare Advantage) — private insurance plans that bundle Parts A and B (and usually D). Approved by Medicare. Alternative to traditional Medicare.
  • Part D (Prescription Drug Coverage) — covers prescription drugs. Sold by private insurers. Requires a monthly premium.
Core Concept
Social Security Survivors Benefits
When a fully or currently insured worker dies, the following may be eligible for survivors benefits:
  • Surviving spouse age 60+ (or 50+ if disabled)
  • Surviving spouse any age if caring for the worker's child under age 16
  • Unmarried children under age 18 (or 19 if still in high school)
  • Dependent parents age 62+
Lump-sum death benefit: $255 paid to surviving spouse (or children if no surviving spouse). This is a tested number — and it's intentionally small.

Exam Tip
Fully Insured vs. Currently Insured
Fully insured (40 credits) unlocks: retirement benefits, full survivors benefits, disability benefits, and Medicare. Currently insured (6 of last 13 credits) unlocks only limited survivors benefits — it does NOT give retirement or disability. A young worker who dies with 6 recent credits still provides survivors benefits for their family even though they weren't fully insured.
Watch Out
Social Security Does NOT Replace Life Insurance
A common exam scenario: the $255 Social Security lump-sum death benefit and survivors benefits are real, but they are rarely sufficient to replace a worker's income for a family. This is why life insurance exists — to fill the gap. The exam may ask you to identify what Social Security covers and what it does NOT. Know that Social Security survivors benefits have limits and eligibility rules that leave many families underprotected.
The Blackout Period — Critical Exam Topic
Core Concept
What Is the Blackout Period?
  • A gap during which a surviving spouse receives NO Social Security survivor benefits
  • Begins: when the youngest child turns age 16 (child SS benefits stop at 18)
  • Ends: when the surviving spouse reaches age 60 (widow/widower benefits resume)
  • Gap can last 10–15+ years depending on family ages
  • Life insurance proceeds invested at the insured's death fill this income gap
Exam Tip
Blackout Period Key Dates
"When does the blackout period BEGIN?" → youngest child turns 16. "When does it END?" → surviving spouse reaches age 60. Exception: surviving spouse with a disability may receive benefits at age 50. Both dates appear regularly on the exam — memorize both.
Social Security Benefit Taxation
Key Numbers

0%SS benefits tax-free if combined income is below the first threshold
Core Concept
Combined Income Formula
Combined Income = AGI + Tax-Exempt Interest + 50% of SS Benefits
  • Below $25K (single) / $32K (married): SS is completely tax-free
  • $25K–$34K (single) / $32K–$44K (married): up to 50% of SS benefits taxable
  • Above $34K (single) / $44K (married): up to 85% of SS benefits taxable
  • Maximum taxable: 85% — never 100% of SS benefits is taxable
Earnings Test & Special Rules
Core Concept
Earnings Test (Pre-FRA)
  • Before FRA: $1 withheld for every $2 earned above the annual limit
  • The year you reach FRA: $1 withheld for every $3 above a higher limit
  • At FRA and beyond: earnings test eliminated — no benefit reduction regardless of income
  • Withheld amounts are credited back once you reach FRA (benefit is recalculated upward)
Core Concept
Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)
Applies to workers who receive a pension from a job NOT covered by Social Security (e.g., some state/local government jobs, foreign employment). Reduces — but does NOT eliminate — the worker's SS retirement benefit using a modified formula. Does NOT apply to survivor benefits or disability benefits (in most cases). The reduction is permanent once applied.
Watch Out
WEP Reduces, Not Eliminates
WEP reduces SS retirement benefits — it does NOT eliminate them entirely. The exam may try to say WEP "eliminates" the benefit. Also: WEP applies to retirement benefits, not survivor benefits. If the question asks about a surviving spouse, WEP is not the answer.
SS Taxation & WEP — Exam Priority Cards
Core Concept
Social Security Benefit Taxation
SS benefits MAY be partially taxable based on combined income (AGI + tax-exempt interest + ½ of SS benefits):
  • Below $25,000 (single) / $32,000 (married): SS benefits NOT taxable
  • $25,000–$34,000 (single) / $32,000–$44,000 (married): up to 50% of SS taxable
  • Above $34,000 (single) / $44,000 (married): up to 85% of SS taxable
KEY RULE: SS benefits are NOT always tax-free. Maximum taxable = 85% (never 100%).
Core Concept
Windfall Elimination Provision (WEP)
Reduces Social Security retirement benefits for workers who receive a pension from employment NOT covered by Social Security (e.g., certain government jobs, state/local employees who did not pay into SS). The standard SS benefit formula gives a higher replacement rate to lower earners — WEP adjusts this for those who appear to be "lower earners" only because some of their work wasn't covered by SS. KEY RULE: WEP reduces SS benefits for people who also receive a non-SS pension from uncovered employment.
