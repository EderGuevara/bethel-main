CHAPTER 10
Social Security & Medicare
Learn the Social Security program — FICA taxes, eligibility, retirement and disability benefits, survivors benefits — and the four parts of Medicare. These concepts directly tie to why life insurance and annuities are needed.
Key Terms
FICA
Federal Insurance Contributions Act. The payroll tax that funds Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%). Total: 7.65% each for employee and employer.
Fully Insured
Worker has earned 40 quarters of coverage (10 years of work). Required for full retirement benefits and most Social Security programs.
Currently Insured
Worker has earned at least 6 quarters of coverage in the last 13 quarters. Qualifies for limited survivors and disability benefits only.
Quarter of Coverage (Credit)
One credit earned for a set amount of earnings. Maximum 4 credits per year. 40 total credits = fully insured.
Full Retirement Age (FRA)
Age at which full Social Security retirement benefits are payable. Currently 66–67 depending on birth year.
Early Retirement
Social Security retirement benefits can begin as early as age 62 — but at a permanently reduced amount (up to 30% reduction).
Delayed Retirement
Delaying benefits past FRA increases monthly benefit by 8% per year (up to age 70).
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Social Security Benefits — 4 Types
Social Security provides four main categories of benefits:
  • Retirement benefits — for fully insured workers (40 credits). Begin at 62 (reduced) or FRA (full). Increased for each year delayed past FRA up to age 70.
  • Disability benefits (SSDI) — for fully insured workers (with additional recent-work requirement). Must be unable to perform any substantial work for at least 12 months. 5-month waiting period before benefits start.
  • Survivors benefits — paid to the spouse and children of a deceased fully or currently insured worker. A lump-sum death benefit of $255 is also paid to the surviving spouse or children.
  • Medicare — health insurance program for those age 65+ and certain disabled individuals.
Core Concept
Medicare — 4 Parts
Medicare has four distinct parts:
  • Part A (Hospital Insurance) — covers inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing, hospice. Funded by FICA taxes — no monthly premium for most people (if they have 40 work credits).
  • Part B (Medical Insurance) — covers outpatient doctor visits, lab tests, preventive care. Requires a monthly premium. Voluntary enrollment.
  • Part C (Medicare Advantage) — private insurance plans that bundle Parts A and B (and usually D). Approved by Medicare. Alternative to traditional Medicare.
  • Part D (Prescription Drug Coverage) — covers prescription drugs. Sold by private insurers. Requires a monthly premium.
Core Concept
Social Security Survivors Benefits
When a fully or currently insured worker dies, the following may be eligible for survivors benefits:
  • Surviving spouse age 60+ (or 50+ if disabled)
  • Surviving spouse any age if caring for the worker's child under age 16
  • Unmarried children under age 18 (or 19 if still in high school)
  • Dependent parents age 62+
Lump-sum death benefit: $255 paid to surviving spouse (or children if no surviving spouse). This is a tested number — and it's intentionally small.

Exam Tip
Fully Insured vs. Currently Insured
Fully insured (40 credits) unlocks: retirement benefits, full survivors benefits, disability benefits, and Medicare. Currently insured (6 of last 13 credits) unlocks only limited survivors benefits — it does NOT give retirement or disability. A young worker who dies with 6 recent credits still provides survivors benefits for their family even though they weren't fully insured.
Watch Out
Social Security Does NOT Replace Life Insurance
A common exam scenario: the $255 Social Security lump-sum death benefit and survivors benefits are real, but they are rarely sufficient to replace a worker's income for a family. This is why life insurance exists — to fill the gap. The exam may ask you to identify what Social Security covers and what it does NOT. Know that Social Security survivors benefits have limits and eligibility rules that leave many families underprotected.
Quick Recall — Must Know Before Moving On
FICA = 7.65% total: 6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare (paid by employee AND employer).
40 credits = fully insured (10 years). 6 of last 13 credits = currently insured.
Retirement: age 62 (reduced), FRA (full), age 70 (maximum — 8%/year increase for delay).
SSDI = 5-month waiting period; disability must be expected to last 12+ months.
Lump-sum death benefit = $255. Small but tested.
Medicare: Part A = hospital (usually free), Part B = medical (premium), Part C = Advantage, Part D = drugs.
Medicare eligibility = age 65 (or disabled after 24 months of SSDI).
