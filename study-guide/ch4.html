CHAPTER 4
Life Insurance Policies
Understand the major types of life insurance policies — term, whole life, universal life, and variable products — their structures, uses, and key differences.
Key Terms
Term Life Insurance
Pure death protection for a specified period. No cash value. Lowest cost per dollar of coverage. "Renting" insurance.
Whole Life Insurance
Permanent coverage for the insured's entire life. Builds cash value. Premiums are level. "Buying" insurance.
Cash Value
The savings element in permanent policies. Grows tax-deferred. Policyholder can borrow against it or surrender for it.
Universal Life (UL)
Flexible-premium permanent insurance. Policyholder can adjust premium and death benefit. Earns current interest rate.
Variable Life
Permanent policy where cash value is invested in separate accounts (like mutual funds). Investment risk borne by policyholder.
Variable Universal Life
Combines flexible premiums of UL with investment options of variable life. Requires both life and securities licenses.
Endowment Policy
Pays face amount if insured dies OR survives to a specified date. Builds cash value rapidly. Rarely sold today.
Credit Life Insurance
Decreasing term insurance tied to a loan balance. Pays off the loan if the borrower dies. Lender is the beneficiary.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Term Life Insurance — Types
Term insurance is temporary coverage — no cash value, just a death benefit. There are three types:
  • Level term — death benefit stays the same throughout the term (most common)
  • Decreasing term — death benefit decreases over time; premium stays level. Used for mortgage protection.
  • Increasing term — death benefit increases over time; often used as a rider, not a standalone policy
Term policies can be renewable (renew without new medical exam, but at higher premium) and/or convertible (convert to permanent without evidence of insurability).
Core Concept
Whole Life Insurance — Types
All whole life policies provide permanent coverage and build cash value. They differ in how premiums are paid:
  • Ordinary (straight) whole life — premiums paid for life; lowest annual premium
  • Limited pay whole life — premiums paid for a fixed period (e.g., 20-pay life, paid-up at 65); higher annual premium but shorter payment period
  • Single premium whole life — one lump-sum payment; policy is immediately paid up; creates a Modified Endowment Contract (MEC)
  • Modified whole life — lower premiums for first few years, then higher level premium for life
Core Concept
Universal Life — How It Works
Universal life has two components inside the policy:
  • Protection element — pure insurance (term) cost, deducted monthly from cash value
  • Savings element (accumulation account) — earns a current interest rate (with a guaranteed minimum, often 3–4%)
The policyholder can increase or decrease premiums (within limits) and adjust the death benefit. If cash value is exhausted from underpayment, the policy lapses. Two death benefit options:
  • Option A (level) — death benefit stays level; as cash value grows, pure insurance needed decreases
  • Option B (increasing) — death benefit = face amount + cash value; more expensive
Core Concept
Variable Products — Key Rule
Any policy where the cash value is invested in separate accounts (equities, bonds, mutual funds) is a variable product. Because these are securities, agents must hold:
  • A state life insurance license
  • A federal securities registration (FINRA Series 6 or 7)
Variable products offer the highest potential return but the policyholder bears the investment risk — the death benefit and cash value can go down.

Exam Tip
Modified Endowment Contract (MEC)
A MEC is a life insurance policy that has been overfunded — too much premium paid too fast (fails the 7-pay test). It loses some tax advantages: withdrawals and loans are taxed LIFO (earnings first) and may incur a 10% penalty before age 59½. The death benefit is still income-tax-free. Single premium whole life always becomes a MEC.
Watch Out
Variable Products Need a Securities License
Fixed life products (term, whole life, UL) require only a state life insurance license. Variable products (variable life, variable universal life, variable annuities) require BOTH a life insurance license AND a securities registration. Selling variable products without a securities license is a serious violation.
Quick Recall — Must Know Before Moving On
Term = temporary, no cash value, pure protection. Whole life = permanent, builds cash value.
Decreasing term = death benefit decreases; credit life is a common use.
Term is renewable (without medical exam) and convertible (to permanent without evidence of insurability).
Whole life cash value grows tax-deferred and equals face amount at age 100 (or 121 for modern policies).
Universal life = flexible premiums + adjustable death benefit + current interest rate on cash value.
Variable products = cash value in separate accounts = investment risk on the policyholder.
Variable products require life license + securities registration (Series 6 or 7).
MEC = overfunded policy (fails 7-pay test); loses preferred tax treatment on withdrawals.
