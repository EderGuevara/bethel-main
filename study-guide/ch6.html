CHAPTER 6
Life Insurance Premiums, Proceeds & Beneficiaries
Understand how premiums are calculated and paid, how death benefits are received tax-free, and the rules governing beneficiary designations, assignments, and policy loans.
Blackout Period
Gap in Social Security survivor benefits; begins when the youngest child turns 16, ends when the surviving spouse reaches age 60; life insurance fills this income gap
Uniform Simultaneous Death Act
If insured and beneficiary die in the same event and order of death cannot be determined, the insured is presumed to have survived the beneficiary; proceeds go to contingent beneficiary or insured's estate
Facility of Payment Clause
Group life insurance provision allowing the insurer to pay a small amount to a relative or funeral provider if no beneficiary can be located
Class Designation
Naming a group as beneficiary (e.g., "my children") rather than specific individuals; membership determined at the time of the insured's death
Tertiary Beneficiary
Third-level beneficiary who receives proceeds only if both the primary and contingent beneficiaries have predeceased the insured
Key Terms
Mortality Factor
The likelihood of death based on age, gender, and health. Higher mortality = higher premium.
Net Single Premium
The present value of future benefits — the theoretical one-time cost of a policy based on mortality and interest only.
Loading
Amount added to the net premium to cover the insurer's expenses, profit, and contingencies.
Gross Premium
What the policyholder actually pays. Net premium + loading = gross premium.
Primary Beneficiary
The first in line to receive the death benefit. Can be one person or multiple people.
Contingent Beneficiary
Receives proceeds ONLY if the primary beneficiary predeceases the insured or cannot receive the benefit.
Revocable Beneficiary
Can be changed by the policyholder at any time without the beneficiary's consent. Most common.
Irrevocable Beneficiary
Cannot be changed without the beneficiary's written consent. Policyholder needs their permission to change beneficiary, take loans, or surrender the policy.
Spendthrift Clause
Prevents the beneficiary from assigning or borrowing against their future benefits. Protects proceeds from creditors.
Policy Loan
Borrowing against the cash value of a permanent policy. NOT taxable. If not repaid, reduces the death benefit.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Factors That Affect Premium Cost
Life insurance premiums are based on three factors:
  • Mortality — probability of death; older or unhealthier people pay more
  • Interest — earnings the insurer expects on invested premiums; higher interest = lower premium
  • Expenses (loading) — insurer's operating costs, commissions, profit margin
Other factors: age at issue, gender, amount of coverage, type of policy, tobacco use, and health classification.
Core Concept
Premium Payment Modes
Policyholders can choose how often to pay. The less frequently, the more they pay overall (insurer charges for the convenience of spreading payments):
  • Annual — lowest total cost; pay once per year
  • Semi-annual — twice a year
  • Quarterly — four times a year
  • Monthly — highest total cost; most common (often auto-drafted)
Core Concept
Tax Treatment of Life Insurance
Life insurance has some of the best tax advantages available:
  • Death benefit — generally income-tax FREE to the beneficiary
  • Cash value growth — grows TAX-DEFERRED; no annual tax on gains
  • Policy loans — NOT taxable as long as the policy stays in force
  • Dividends — NOT taxable until they exceed total premiums paid
  • 1035 exchange — exchange one policy for another without triggering taxes
Exception: employer-paid group life over $50,000 in coverage — the value of the excess is taxable to the employee.
Core Concept
Beneficiary Designations
How proceeds are distributed depends on who is named and how:
  • Per capita — divide equally among all surviving named beneficiaries
  • Per stirpes — if a named beneficiary dies, their share passes to their children (heirs)
  • Estate as beneficiary — proceeds go through probate; often taxable as part of estate; generally avoid
  • Minor as beneficiary — proceeds must be managed by a court-appointed guardian or trust; direct naming of minors is usually discouraged

Exam Tip
Policy Loans Are NOT Taxable Income
When a policyholder borrows from their cash value, it is a loan — not a withdrawal. Loans are not taxable income regardless of size, as long as the policy remains in force. If the policy lapses or is surrendered with an outstanding loan, the loan amount becomes taxable to the extent it exceeds the cost basis (total premiums paid).
Exam Tip
1035 Exchange — Tax-Free Policy Swap
Under Section 1035 of the Internal Revenue Code, you can exchange:
  • Life insurance → life insurance or annuity (tax-free)
  • Annuity → annuity (tax-free)
  • Annuity → life insurance (NOT allowed tax-free)
The exchange must be done directly between insurers — you cannot receive the proceeds and then buy a new policy. 1035 preserves the tax basis and avoids triggering a taxable event.
Watch Out
Naming Your Estate as Beneficiary
If the estate is named as beneficiary (or if there is no beneficiary), the death benefit passes through probate. This means: delays in payment, public record, creditor claims against the estate, and potential estate taxes. Always name a specific person as beneficiary to keep proceeds out of the estate and paid quickly.
Special Beneficiary Rules
Core Concept
Uniform Simultaneous Death Act
If the insured and beneficiary die in the same accident and it cannot be determined who died first, the law presumes the insured survived the beneficiary. Result: proceeds go to the contingent beneficiary, or if none, to the insured's estate — NOT to the deceased primary beneficiary's estate. Most policies also include a Common Disaster Clause requiring the beneficiary to survive the insured by a specified period (e.g., 30 days) to collect.
Exam Tip
Simultaneous Death Scenario
"Insured and spouse die in a car crash — order of death unknown. Who gets the proceeds?" → Insured presumed to survive → proceeds go to contingent beneficiary. If no contingent beneficiary exists, proceeds go to insured's estate. NOT to the spouse's estate. This appears on nearly every licensing exam.
Core Concept
Class Designation
  • Beneficiary identified as a group ("my children," "my grandchildren") rather than specific names
  • Members are determined at the time of the insured's death — not at policy issue
  • Children born or adopted after the policy is issued ARE included in "my children"
  • Per stirpes vs. per capita rules apply to distributions within the class
Core Concept
Facility of Payment Clause
Found in group life insurance certificates. Allows the insurer to pay up to a small amount (typically $2,000) to a relative or person who paid for the insured's funeral or final illness, IF: no beneficiary has been designated, OR the named beneficiary cannot be located, OR the beneficiary is incompetent. Common in group plans to handle cases where beneficiary forms are never updated.
The Blackout Period — Life Insurance Need
Core Concept
Social Security Blackout Period
  • Begins: when the youngest child turns age 16 (child SS benefits stop at age 18)
  • Ends: when the surviving spouse reaches age 60 (when widow/widower SS benefits resume)
  • During this period, the surviving spouse has NO Social Security income
  • Gap can last 10–15+ years for young families
  • Life insurance proceeds invested at the insured's death can fill this income gap — this is one of life insurance's most important personal uses
Exam Tip
Blackout Period Key Dates
Blackout period starts when youngest child turns 16 (not 18, not at death). Blackout period ends when surviving spouse reaches age 60. Exception: surviving spouse with disability may receive benefits at age 50. The exam will ask both the start and end dates — memorize both.
Class Designation, Minor as Beneficiary & Simultaneous Death
Core Concept
Class Designation
Naming a GROUP rather than specific individuals as beneficiary (e.g., "my children," "my grandchildren"). Advantage: automatically includes future members of that class (e.g., a child born after the policy is issued is automatically included). No need to update the policy when a new family member arrives.
Watch Out
Minor as Beneficiary
Minors CANNOT legally receive life insurance proceeds directly. The insurer will hold or pay proceeds to a court-appointed guardian of the minor's estate — a slow, expensive process. Better solutions: name a trustee, establish a trust, or designate a custodian under UTMA. KEY RULE: Minors can't directly receive proceeds — plan around this with a trust or trustee.
Core Concept
Uniform Simultaneous Death Act (USDA)
If the insured and beneficiary die simultaneously (e.g., common accident) and it cannot be determined who died first — the insured is presumed to have survived the beneficiary. RESULT: Proceeds go to the CONTINGENT beneficiary (or insured's estate), NOT the primary beneficiary's estate. KEY RULE: Simultaneous death → insured presumed to survive → contingent beneficiary receives proceeds.
