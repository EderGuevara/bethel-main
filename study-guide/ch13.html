CHAPTER 13
State Regulations, Licensing & Ethics
Understand how agents are licensed and regulated, the powers of the Insurance Commissioner, agent authority, and the full list of unfair trade practices that the exam tests. This chapter represents 15–20% of most state exams.
Key Terms
Insurance Commissioner
The state official who regulates the insurance industry. Appointed by the governor or elected. Heads the Department (Division) of Insurance.
Producer
The legal term for any licensed insurance salesperson — includes agents and brokers. Used interchangeably with "agent" on the exam.
Appointment
Authorization from an insurer allowing a licensed producer to sell on their behalf. Must be filed with the state DOI by the insurer (not the agent).
Continuing Education (CE)
Required ongoing education to renew a license. MA requires 24 CE hours per 2-year renewal cycle, including 3 hours of ethics.
Rebating
Giving or offering anything of value to a prospect as an inducement to buy insurance — including returning part of the commission. Illegal in most states.
Twisting
Inducing a policyholder to replace an existing policy at a different company through misrepresentation or incomplete comparison. Illegal.
Churning
Same as twisting but within the same insurance company. Illegal. Both practices harm the consumer.
Fiduciary
A person who holds a position of trust. Agents hold client premiums in a fiduciary capacity — they must forward premiums to the insurer promptly.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
The Insurance Commissioner's Powers
The Commissioner (or Superintendent) of Insurance has broad authority over the insurance market in their state:
  • License insurers and producers — and revoke/suspend those licenses
  • Approve policy forms and premium rates before they can be used
  • Investigate consumer complaints and conduct market conduct examinations
  • Enforce state insurance laws and assess fines and penalties
  • Rehabilitate or liquidate insolvent insurers (place them in receivership)
  • Issue cease and desist orders to stop illegal practices immediately
The Commissioner cannot override federal law. Insurance is primarily state-regulated, but federal law (ERISA, securities laws) takes precedence in specific areas.
Core Concept
Agent Licensing Requirements
To become a licensed insurance producer, a person must:
  • Be at least 18 years old
  • Complete pre-licensing education (required hours vary by state and line of authority)
  • Pass the state licensing exam for the lines of authority sought
  • Submit an application and pay the licensing fee
  • Pass a background check (criminal history can disqualify)
After licensing, the producer must be appointed by an insurer before selling that company's products. A license without an appointment is inactive for that company. Producers must be licensed in each state where they transact business (with some reciprocal licensing agreements between states).
Core Concept
Types of Agent Authority
The relationship between an agent and the insurer is governed by contract law. Three types of authority:
  • Express authority — authority explicitly written in the agent's contract with the insurer (e.g., "you may bind coverage up to $X")
  • Implied authority — authority not written but reasonably assumed to exist based on the agent's role (e.g., an agent can accept a premium even if the contract doesn't specifically say so)
  • Apparent authority — authority that a third party (customer) reasonably believes the agent has, based on the insurer's conduct. If the insurer allows the agent to act a certain way, the insurer may be bound even if it wasn't authorized
Core Concept
Unfair Trade Practices — The Full List
These are illegal acts that agents and insurers are prohibited from doing. Know every one:
  • Misrepresentation — making false or misleading statements about any policy (yours or a competitor's)
  • False advertising — deceptive ads or sales materials
  • Defamation — making false statements about another insurer's financial condition or character
  • Rebating — offering anything of value (money, gifts, extra services) as an inducement to buy insurance
  • Twisting — replacing a competitor's policy through misrepresentation
  • Churning — replacing the same company's policy through misrepresentation
  • Coercion / intimidation — using threats, force, or pressure to sell or prevent a sale
  • Unfair discrimination — treating applicants differently based on protected characteristics (race, color, national origin, religion, sex)
  • Boycott — conspiring with others to restrict competition
  • Unfair claims settlement practices — unreasonably delaying or denying valid claims

Core Concept
Unfair Discrimination vs. Lawful Discrimination
Not all differences in treatment are illegal. The exam tests the distinction:
  • Unlawful discrimination — treating applicants differently based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, or marital status. Never allowed.
  • Lawful discrimination — treating applicants differently based on valid actuarial risk factors:
    • Age — older people pay more (higher mortality)
    • Health status / medical history
    • Tobacco use
    • Occupation / hazardous activities
    • Gender (in most states — some states require unisex rates)
Insurance is all about pricing risk accurately. Charging a 60-year-old more than a 30-year-old is lawful discrimination — it reflects real actuarial differences, not prejudice.
Core Concept
Agent Duties and Responsibilities
A licensed producer has legal and ethical duties to both the insurer and the client:
  • Hold premiums in trust — premium payments collected from clients must be forwarded promptly to the insurer; commingling with personal funds is prohibited
  • Accurate applications — agents must not alter, falsify, or submit incomplete applications
  • Suitability — recommend products that are appropriate for the client's needs and financial situation
  • Disclosure — disclose all material facts about a policy; never omit information that would affect the client's decision
  • Notify insurer of changes — if an applicant's health changes between application and policy delivery, the agent must report it
  • Keep records — maintain client records as required by state law (typically 3–5 years)

Exam Tip
Rebating Is Illegal Even If the Client Asks
A common exam scenario: a client asks their agent for a discount or part of the commission back in exchange for purchasing the policy. Even if the client initiates the request, the agent cannot comply. Accepting is rebating. Both the agent AND the client can be penalized in many states. The correct answer: the agent must decline and explain that it is illegal.
Exam Tip
Cease and Desist vs. License Revocation
The Commissioner can issue a cease and desist order immediately to stop an ongoing illegal practice — without a hearing first. License suspension or revocation typically requires a hearing where the agent has the right to respond to charges. Know the order: cease and desist comes first (stop now), then hearing, then possible revocation.
Watch Out
Appointment vs. License — They Are Not the Same
An agent can be licensed without being appointed. A license gives you the legal authority to sell insurance in a state. An appointment gives you authority to sell for a specific insurer. You need BOTH. If an agent's appointment with Company A is terminated, they keep their license and can seek appointment with Company B — they do not lose their license. The insurer files the appointment; the agent files the license application.
