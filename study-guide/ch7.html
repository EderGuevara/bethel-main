CHAPTER 7
Life Insurance Underwriting & Policy Issue
Learn the underwriting process — from application submission through policy delivery — including risk classification, MIB, medical exams, and replacement rules.
Key Terms
Underwriting
The process of evaluating applications to determine insurability and appropriate premium. Controls adverse selection.
MIB (Medical Information Bureau)
A shared database where insurers report medical and lifestyle information from applications. Helps detect fraud and misrepresentation.
Preferred Risk
Healthiest applicants — below-average mortality. Qualify for the lowest premiums.
Standard Risk
Average health — normal life expectancy. Issued at standard rates.
Substandard Risk
Higher-than-average health risk. Issued at higher (rated) premiums or with exclusions.
Declined (Rejected)
Application refused — risk is too high for any rating or the condition is uninsurable.
Conditional Receipt
Given to applicant when first premium is paid at application. Coverage is conditional — begins if the applicant is found insurable as applied for.
Backdating
Dating the policy prior to the application date to get a lower age (and lower premium). Most states allow up to 6 months back.
Replacement
Terminating an existing policy and purchasing a new one. Regulated to prevent churning and protect consumers.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
The Application — Part 1 and Part 2
The life insurance application has two parts:
  • Part 1 — General information: name, address, date of birth, amount of coverage requested, beneficiary, policy type
  • Part 2 — Medical history and health questions: prior illnesses, medications, family history, tobacco use
The completed application becomes part of the policy contract. The applicant must sign the application and attest that all answers are accurate.
Core Concept
Sources of Underwriting Information
Underwriters gather information from multiple sources:
  • Application — health history, lifestyle, finances, beneficiaries
  • Medical exam — required for larger policies; blood tests, urinalysis, blood pressure, height/weight
  • Attending physician statement (APS) — obtained from the applicant's doctor when health history flags an issue
  • MIB report — checks prior applications and reported conditions
  • Inspection report / consumer report — background on finances, habits, and character (governed by FCRA)
Core Concept
Conditional Receipt vs. Binding Receipt
  • Conditional receipt — the most common. Coverage begins on the date of application (or medical exam, if required) IF the applicant is found insurable as applied for. If the applicant is not insurable, there is no coverage and premiums are returned.
  • Binding receipt — provides coverage immediately and unconditionally for a limited time (e.g., 30–60 days). Less common.
Without any receipt, coverage does not begin until the policy is delivered and the first premium is paid.
Core Concept
Policy Delivery Requirements
For the policy to take effect, all of the following must occur:
  • Policy must be delivered to the applicant while they are still in good health
  • First premium must be paid (if not collected at application)
  • Any outstanding requirements (health statements, amended applications) must be satisfied
If the applicant's health has changed since the application, the agent must note this — the insurer may re-underwrite the policy.

Exam Tip
Who Is the Applicant vs. the Insured?
The applicant (owner) is the person applying for and paying for the policy — they have all contractual rights. The insured is the person whose life is covered. They can be the same person, or different (e.g., a parent buying insurance on a child). The agent owes a duty to the applicant/owner, not necessarily the insured.
Exam Tip
Churning vs. Twisting
  • Churning — inducing a policyholder to replace their own company's policy unnecessarily (agent replacing within the same insurer)
  • Twisting — inducing a policyholder to replace a policy from one insurer to another using misrepresentation or incomplete comparisons
Both are illegal and unethical practices. Both are forms of replacement abuse.
Watch Out
Changes Made to the Application
If the agent or insurer makes a material change to the application during underwriting (e.g., adds an exclusion rider or changes the coverage amount), the applicant must receive a counter-offer — the new policy — and has the right to accept or reject it. The original application is no longer valid. The policyholder should never be bound to a policy they didn't apply for.
Quick Recall — Must Know Before Moving On
Underwriting purpose = evaluate risk to control adverse selection and set appropriate premiums.
Application Part 1 = general info. Part 2 = health/medical history.
MIB = database of prior insurance applications — helps detect fraud.
Conditional receipt = coverage backdates to application date IF applicant is insurable as applied for.
Risk classes: Preferred → Standard → Substandard (rated) → Declined.
Policy effective only when delivered, in good health, first premium paid.
Backdating = dating policy prior to application to save age; most states allow up to 6 months.
Twisting = replacing a competitor's policy with misrepresentation. Churning = same insurer.
Replacement requires: Notice of Replacement, policy comparison, notify existing insurer.
