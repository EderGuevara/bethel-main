CHAPTER 11
Retirement Plans
Master the landscape of retirement plans — qualified vs. non-qualified, ERISA, IRAs, 401(k)s, SEPs, SIMPLEs, and the rules around contributions, distributions, RMDs, and rollovers.
Keogh Plan (HR-10)
Qualified retirement plan for self-employed individuals (sole proprietors and partners); contribution limits equal to corporate qualified plans
Profit Sharing Plan
Defined contribution plan where employer contributions are discretionary — vary based on profits; employer may contribute $0 in a given year
Money Purchase Plan
Defined contribution plan with FIXED annual employer contributions stated as a percentage of compensation; contributions are mandatory every year regardless of profits
Cliff Vesting
Vesting schedule where the employee is 0% vested until a specific date, then becomes 100% vested all at once (maximum 3 years under ERISA)
Graded Vesting
Vesting schedule where the employee's ownership of employer contributions increases gradually over time (must reach 100% by year 6 under ERISA)
Section 72(t) — SEPP
IRS provision allowing substantially equal periodic payments from a retirement account before age 59½ without the 10% early withdrawal penalty
Key Terms
Qualified Plan
A retirement plan that meets IRS/ERISA requirements. Contributions are tax-deductible; growth is tax-deferred; distributions taxed as ordinary income.
Non-Qualified Plan
Does not meet IRS requirements. Funded with after-tax dollars. Only earnings are taxed at distribution. More flexible — not bound by ERISA limits.
ERISA
Employee Retirement Income Security Act (1974). Federal law governing qualified plans — sets standards for participation, vesting, funding, and fiduciary duty.
Traditional IRA
Individual Retirement Account — contributions may be tax-deductible. Grows tax-deferred. Taxed as ordinary income at withdrawal.
Roth IRA
Funded with after-tax dollars. Growth and qualified withdrawals are TAX-FREE. No RMDs during the owner's lifetime.
RMD (Required Minimum Distribution)
Mandatory withdrawals from qualified plans and traditional IRAs beginning at age 73. Failure to take RMDs = 25% excise tax on the amount not taken.
Vesting
The process by which an employee earns a non-forfeitable right to employer contributions. Immediate, cliff, or graded vesting schedules.
Rollover
Moving funds from one retirement plan to another. Direct rollover = tax-free. Indirect rollover = 60-day window; if late, it's a taxable distribution.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Qualified vs. Non-Qualified Plans
The core distinction on the exam:
  • Qualified plans — meet IRS/ERISA rules; contributions are pre-tax (employer deductible + employee pre-tax); grow tax-deferred; distributions taxed as ordinary income; subject to contribution limits and RMDs
  • Non-qualified plans — no IRS approval required; funded with after-tax money; more flexibility on design and eligibility; only earnings are taxed at distribution; NOT subject to ERISA or IRS contribution limits
Non-qualified plans are often used for executive compensation (deferred comp, executive bonus, split dollar).
Core Concept
IRAs — Traditional vs. Roth
  • Traditional IRA — deductible contributions (if income limits allow); tax-deferred growth; taxed at withdrawal; RMDs at age 73. Best if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket in retirement.
  • Roth IRA — after-tax contributions (no deduction); TAX-FREE growth and withdrawals (if qualified — account open 5+ years, age 59½+); NO RMDs ever. Best if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement.
Contribution limit (same for both in 2024): $7,000/year ($8,000 if age 50+). Income limits apply to Roth IRA contributions.
Core Concept
Employer-Sponsored Plans
  • 401(k) — private sector. Employee contributes pre-tax; employer may match. 2024 limit: $23,000 ($30,500 if 50+).
  • 403(b) — non-profit and public education employees. Similar to 401(k).
  • 457 — government and some non-profit employees. No 10% early withdrawal penalty.
  • SEP-IRA — for self-employed/small business; employer contributes only; up to 25% of compensation or $69,000 (2024).
  • SIMPLE IRA — for small businesses (≤100 employees); both employee and employer contribute; lower limits than 401(k).

Exam Tip
Traditional IRA vs. Roth IRA Tax Timing
The key phrase: pay taxes now (Roth) or pay taxes later (Traditional). With Traditional IRA you get a deduction today but pay tax when you withdraw. With Roth you get no deduction today but pay zero tax on qualified withdrawals. The Roth also has no RMDs — making it ideal for estate planning. When the exam says "tax-free growth and distributions," that's the Roth.
Exam Tip
Section 457 Has No Early Withdrawal Penalty
Unlike 401(k) and IRA plans, a Section 457 governmental deferred compensation plan does NOT have a 10% early withdrawal penalty. This makes it unique. Early withdrawals from a 457 plan are still taxed as ordinary income — they just avoid the 10% penalty. This is a common exam trap.
Watch Out
Qualified Plan Doesn't Mean Tax-Free
"Qualified" means the plan meets IRS requirements for tax advantages — it does NOT mean tax-free. It means tax-DEFERRED. Distributions from qualified plans (traditional IRA, 401k, 403b) are taxed as ordinary income. Only Roth-type accounts offer truly tax-free distributions (on the qualified portion). Don't confuse qualified with tax-free on the exam.
Keogh Plans (HR-10) — Self-Employed Retirement
Core Concept
Keogh Plan Overview
  • Available to self-employed individuals: sole proprietors and partners (NOT S-corp shareholders acting as employees)
  • Contribution limits equal those of corporate qualified plans: up to 25% of compensation or $69,000 (2024), depending on plan type
  • If the owner has common-law employees, contributions must also be made for them
  • Can be structured as a defined benefit or defined contribution plan
  • More complex to administer than a SEP-IRA, but allows higher contributions in some scenarios
Watch Out
Keogh Eligibility — Self-Employed Only
Keogh plans are for self-employed sole proprietors and partners ONLY. If a business is incorporated, the owner is an employee and uses a corporate 401(k) or similar plan — NOT a Keogh. The exam will test who qualifies. Incorporated business = no Keogh.
Defined Contribution Plan Types
Core Concept
Profit Sharing vs. Money Purchase Plan
  • Profit Sharing: employer contributions are DISCRETIONARY. Can contribute $0 in a bad year. Employees share in company profits when they exist.
  • Money Purchase (Pension) Plan: employer contributions are MANDATORY and FIXED (set percentage of each participant's compensation). Even if the company has zero profits, the employer MUST contribute. Penalty for failure to contribute.
Exam Tip
Which Plan Requires Annual Contributions?
The key difference: Money Purchase = mandatory fixed % every year. Profit Sharing = discretionary (can be $0). "Which plan requires the employer to contribute every year regardless of profits?" → Money Purchase Plan. This is a frequent exam question.
Vesting Schedules
Core Concept
Cliff vs. Graded Vesting
  • Employee's own contributions: always 100% immediately vested — employer cannot take back what the employee contributed
  • Cliff Vesting: 0% vested until a set date, then 100% all at once. ERISA maximum = 3 years (100% after year 3)
  • Graded Vesting: gradual increase over time. ERISA minimum schedule: 20% after year 2, then 20%/year through year 6 (100% by year 6)
  • Vesting applies only to EMPLOYER contributions — never to the employee's own contributions

Watch Out
Forfeiture of Unvested Employer Contributions
If an employee quits before being fully vested, they forfeit the unvested portion of employer contributions. Example: employee is 60% vested and has $10,000 in employer match → keeps $6,000, forfeits $4,000. The employee always keeps 100% of their own contributions.
Exceptions to the 10% Early Withdrawal Penalty
Core Concept
Section 72(t) — Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPPs)
  • Allows withdrawals from an IRA or qualified plan before age 59½ WITHOUT the 10% penalty
  • Payments must be substantially equal and continue for the LONGER of: 5 years OR until age 59½
  • Cannot change the payment schedule once started without triggering a retroactive penalty
  • Three IRS-approved calculation methods: Required Minimum Distribution, Fixed Amortization, Fixed Annuitization
Exam Tip
Other 10% Penalty Exceptions
  • Death or permanent disability
  • Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
  • Qualified higher education expenses (IRAs only)
  • First-time home purchase — up to $10,000 (IRAs only)
  • Health insurance premiums while unemployed (IRAs only)
  • Military reservist called to active duty
  • Qualified birth or adoption — up to $5,000
Defined Benefit vs. Defined Contribution — Exam Priority
Core Concept
Defined Benefit vs. Defined Contribution Plans
  • Defined Benefit (Pension): employer PROMISES a specific benefit amount at retirement (based on salary and years of service). Employer bears ALL investment risk — regardless of how investments perform, the promised benefit is paid.
  • Defined Contribution (401k, 403b, SEP): the CONTRIBUTION amount is defined. The ultimate benefit depends on investment performance. Employee (and sometimes employer) bears the investment risk.
KEY RULE: Defined Benefit = employer promises and bears risk. Defined Contribution = defined input, variable output — employee bears investment risk.
