CHAPTER 14
Transfer for Value, Estate Planning, Disability & LTC
Four critical exam topics that don't fit neatly into one category: the transfer for value tax trap, using life insurance in estate planning, disability income insurance basics, and long-term care insurance.
Part 1 — Transfer for Value Rule
Transfer for Value Rule
When a life insurance policy is sold or transferred for valuable consideration, the death benefit LOSES its income-tax-free status. The excess over basis becomes taxable.
Cost Basis (in this context)
The amount the buyer paid for the policy PLUS any premiums paid after the transfer. Only this amount is received tax-free; everything above it is taxable.
Carryover Basis Transfer
A transfer that is tax-free (gift, divorce, corporate restructure). Does NOT trigger the transfer for value rule — the original tax-free treatment is preserved.
Core Concept
The Transfer for Value Rule — How It Works
Life insurance death benefits are normally 100% income-tax free. The transfer for value rule is the main exception:
  • If a policy is sold or transferred for valuable consideration (money, assumption of a debt, etc.), the death benefit loses its tax-free status
  • The new owner (buyer) will pay ordinary income tax on the death benefit received, minus what they paid for the policy and premiums paid after the transfer
Example: Investor buys a $500,000 policy for $50,000, then pays $10,000 more in premiums. When the insured dies, the investor receives $500,000. Taxable amount = $500,000 − $60,000 = $440,000 taxable as ordinary income.
Core Concept
Exceptions to the Transfer for Value Rule
These transfers do NOT trigger the rule — the death benefit remains tax-free:
  • Transfer to the insured (e.g., employee buys back their own key person policy)
  • Transfer to a partner of the insured
  • Transfer to a partnership in which the insured is a partner
  • Transfer to a corporation in which the insured is a shareholder or officer
  • Transfer where the transferee's basis is determined by the transferor's basis (carryover basis — gifts, certain tax-free corporate transactions)
Memory trick: transfers within a business relationship involving the insured are safe. It's the stranger-owned sale that triggers the rule.
Part 2 — Estate Planning with Life Insurance
Estate Tax Inclusion
If the insured owns their own life insurance policy at death, the full death benefit is included in their taxable estate — even though it's income-tax free to the beneficiary.
ILIT
Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust. A trust that owns the life insurance policy, removing the death benefit from the insured's taxable estate.
Incidents of Ownership
Any rights in a policy (ability to change beneficiary, take loans, surrender). If the insured holds any incidents of ownership at death, the policy IS included in the taxable estate.
Crummey Notice
A written notice sent to trust beneficiaries giving them a brief window to withdraw contributions to the trust. Converts gifts to the trust into annual exclusion gifts (not taxable).
Core Concept
When Life Insurance IS Included in the Taxable Estate
The death benefit is income-tax free — but it can still be subject to estate tax if:
  • The insured owns the policy (is the policyowner) at the time of death
  • The insured holds any incidents of ownership (right to change beneficiary, borrow, surrender)
  • The insured's estate is named as beneficiary
For high-net-worth clients, this can mean a $1,000,000 death benefit adds $1,000,000 to the taxable estate — potentially creating a significant estate tax bill even though the beneficiary received the money income-tax free.
Core Concept
ILIT — Keeping Life Insurance Out of the Estate
An Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) solves the estate tax problem:
  • The trust owns the policy — not the insured. The insured holds no incidents of ownership.
  • The trust is the beneficiary — death benefit goes to the trust, not the insured's estate
  • Death benefit is excluded from the taxable estate of the insured
  • Insured gifts money to the trust to pay premiums — using the annual gift tax exclusion ($18,000 per beneficiary in 2024)
  • Crummey notices are sent to trust beneficiaries to qualify the gifts as annual exclusion gifts
Downside: the trust is irrevocable — the insured gives up all rights to the policy permanently.
Part 3 — Disability Income Insurance
Disability Income (DI)
Insurance that replaces a portion of earned income when the insured cannot work due to illness or injury. Does NOT replace 100% of income (typically 60–70%).
Elimination Period
A waiting period before DI benefits begin — like a deductible measured in time. Common: 30, 60, 90, or 180 days. Longer elimination period = lower premium.
Benefit Period
How long DI benefits are paid. Options: 2 years, 5 years, to age 65, or lifetime. Longer benefit period = higher premium.
Own-Occupation
Disability defined as inability to perform the duties of YOUR specific occupation. Most favorable to the insured. Surgeons, dentists, and specialists favor this definition.
Any-Occupation
Disability defined as inability to perform ANY occupation for which you are reasonably suited by education, training, or experience. More restrictive. Harder to qualify.
Core Concept
Disability Income — Key Features
  • Elimination period = waiting period before benefits start. Acts like a deductible. Longer = lower premium.
  • Benefit period = how long benefits last. "To age 65" is the gold standard for individual DI.
  • Own-occupation definition = best protection; insured is disabled if they can't do their own job
  • Partial/residual disability — proportional benefits when insured can work part-time or at reduced capacity
  • Non-cancelable — insurer cannot cancel or raise premiums as long as premiums are paid (best contract)
  • Guaranteed renewable — insurer must renew; can raise premiums for entire risk class but not one individual
Key Concept
DI Taxation — Who Pays the Premium Matters
Employee pays premiumBenefits received are tax-free
Employer pays premiumBenefits received are taxable income to the employee
Employer + employee shareBenefits are partially taxable (proportional to employer's share)
Part 4 — Long-Term Care Insurance
Long-Term Care (LTC)
Insurance that covers custodial care services — assistance with Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) — in a nursing home, assisted living facility, or at home.
Activities of Daily Living (ADLs)
The 6 basic self-care functions: Bathing, Dressing, Eating, Toileting, Transferring (moving in/out of bed), Continence. Need help with 2+ ADLs typically triggers LTC benefits.
Cognitive Impairment
Severe cognitive deterioration (e.g., Alzheimer's disease) is an alternative trigger for LTC benefits, even if the insured can perform ADLs physically.
Inflation Protection Rider
Increases the daily/monthly benefit over time to keep pace with rising care costs. Highly recommended — nursing home costs increase significantly over time.
Partnership Program
State-federal program: if a qualified LTC policy pays out a certain amount, the state allows the insured to protect an equivalent amount of assets from Medicaid spend-down requirements.
Core Concept
LTC Insurance — How It Works
Long-term care insurance is designed to cover what Medicare and regular health insurance do NOT cover — custodial (non-medical) care:
  • Benefit triggers — benefits begin when insured can't perform 2+ of the 6 ADLs, OR has severe cognitive impairment
  • Elimination period — waiting period before benefits begin (30, 60, or 90 days most common)
  • Daily/monthly benefit — the dollar amount paid per day or month for care
  • Benefit period — how long benefits are paid (2 years, 3 years, 5 years, lifetime)
  • Setting — can cover nursing home, assisted living, adult day care, home health care

Exam Tip
Medicare Does NOT Cover Custodial Care
This is one of the most tested facts about LTC. Medicare covers skilled medical care (nurses, therapists) — not custodial care (help bathing, dressing, etc.). The #1 reason people need LTC insurance is that Medicare will NOT pay for the kind of care most people need as they age. Medicaid WILL pay for nursing home care — but only after the person has spent down almost all their assets.
Watch Out
Transfer for Value — Business Exceptions Are Narrow
The exceptions to the transfer for value rule are specific and narrow. A transfer to a co-shareholder (not the insured) does NOT qualify for an exception. A transfer to a trust does NOT qualify (unless the insured is the grantor and the trust is a grantor trust). When in doubt: if the transfer involves a stranger or a third party with no business relationship to the insured, assume the transfer for value rule applies and the death benefit will be partially taxable.
LTC Partnership Program — Exam Priority
Core Concept
LTC Partnership Program
A state-federal collaboration that encourages people to buy private LTC insurance. How it works: after a qualified LTC policy has paid benefits up to a certain dollar amount, the state allows the insured to PROTECT an equal dollar amount of personal assets from Medicaid's spend-down requirements.

Example: a $200,000 LTC policy pays out its full benefit → insured can now protect $200,000 of assets and still qualify for Medicaid.

KEY RULE: Partnership Program = dollar-for-dollar asset protection from Medicaid spend-down, equal to the amount the LTC policy paid out.
Quick Recall — Must Know Before Moving On
Transfer for value: selling a policy for consideration = death benefit partially taxable. Exception: transfers to the insured, partner, partnership, or corporation where insured is shareholder/officer.
Estate tax: insured who OWNS their own policy has death benefit included in taxable estate.
ILIT: trust owns the policy → removes death benefit from insured's taxable estate.
Incidents of ownership (right to borrow, change beneficiary, surrender) = policy included in estate.
DI elimination period = waiting period before benefits start. Longer = lower premium.
Own-occupation = can't do YOUR job = disabled. Any-occupation = can't do ANY job = disabled.
DI taxation: employee pays = benefits tax-free. Employer pays = benefits taxable to employee.
LTC triggers: unable to perform 2+ of 6 ADLs, OR severe cognitive impairment.
6 ADLs: Bathing, Dressing, Eating, Toileting, Transferring, Continence.
Medicare does NOT cover custodial care (ADL help) — LTC insurance fills this gap.
