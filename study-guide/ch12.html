CHAPTER 12
Uses of Life Insurance
See how life insurance is applied in the real world — for personal protection, business continuity, executive benefits, estate planning, and living benefits like viatical settlements and policy loans.
Key Terms
Key Person Insurance
A business buys insurance on a key employee whose death would cause significant financial loss. Business is owner and beneficiary. Premiums NOT deductible; death benefit tax-free.
Buy-Sell Agreement
A legal agreement between business partners to buy out a deceased partner's share. Life insurance funds the buyout. Ensures business continuity.
Executive Bonus (Sec. 162)
Employer pays premiums on employee-owned life policy as a bonus. Premium is a deductible business expense; taxable income to the employee.
Deferred Compensation
An agreement to pay an executive additional compensation at a future date (usually retirement). Often informally funded with life insurance. Non-qualified plan.
Split-Dollar Life Insurance
Employer and employee share the cost, cash value, and/or death benefit of a life insurance policy. Flexible arrangement for executive benefits.
Viatical Settlement
A terminally ill insured (life expectancy ≤ 2 years) sells their life insurance policy to a third party for a lump sum (less than face value). Proceeds are generally tax-free.
Life Settlement
Similar to viatical but for non-terminally ill policyholders who no longer need or can afford coverage. Usually for seniors. Proceeds may be taxable.
COLI (Corporate-Owned Life Insurance)
A company purchases life insurance on its employees. Death benefit goes to the company. Used to offset benefit costs or fund key person needs.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
Business Uses of Life Insurance
Life insurance is a powerful tool for business planning:
  • Key person insurance — protects the business from the financial impact of losing a critical employee; business owns the policy, business is the beneficiary
  • Buy-sell agreement — uses life insurance to fund the buyout of a deceased owner's share; provides liquidity; keeps the business in the hands of surviving owners
  • Business continuation — ensures the business can survive the death of a principal without liquidation
Core Concept
Buy-Sell Agreement Structures
There are two main structures for buy-sell agreements:
  • Cross-purchase agreement — each owner buys a life insurance policy on every other owner. If there are 3 partners, each buys 2 policies = 6 policies total. When one dies, the survivors use the proceeds to buy the deceased's shares. Each surviving owner's cost basis is increased.
  • Entity purchase (stock redemption) — the business itself owns and is the beneficiary of policies on each owner. The business buys out the deceased owner's shares. Fewer policies; no step-up in cost basis for surviving owners.
Core Concept
Executive Benefit Plans Using Life Insurance
Life insurance is used in several executive compensation strategies:
  • Executive bonus (Section 162) — employer pays life insurance premiums as a bonus; deductible to employer; taxable to executive; executive owns the policy
  • Deferred compensation — promise to pay future income, often funded informally with life insurance; non-qualified; golden handcuffs to retain key employees
  • Split-dollar — cost, cash value, and/or death benefit split between employer and employee; very flexible; two types: endorsement (employer owns) and collateral assignment (employee owns)
Core Concept
Viatical vs. Life Settlements
Both allow a policyholder to sell their life insurance policy to a third-party investor for cash. The investor pays the premiums and collects the death benefit when the insured dies.
  • Viatical settlement — insured is terminally ill (life expectancy ≤ 2 years); proceeds generally tax-free under federal law
  • Life settlement — insured is NOT terminally ill; usually elderly; proceeds may be taxable (gain above cost basis is ordinary income; additional gain may be capital gains)
Both pay MORE than the cash surrender value but LESS than the face amount.

Exam Tip
Cross-Purchase vs. Entity Buy-Sell
The exam loves to test how many policies are needed. Formula for cross-purchase: N × (N − 1) where N = number of partners. 2 partners = 2 policies. 3 partners = 6 policies. 4 partners = 12 policies. Entity purchase always = one policy per partner (the business owns all of them). Entity purchase = simpler administration; cross-purchase = better tax basis for survivors.
Exam Tip
Accelerated Death Benefit vs. Viatical Settlement
Both provide living benefits to a terminally ill insured, but they are different:
  • Accelerated death benefit rider — paid directly by the insurer as an advance on the death benefit; no third party; insured keeps remaining death benefit
  • Viatical settlement — insured SELLS the policy to a third-party investor; insured receives cash; investor gets the full death benefit when insured dies
Accelerated benefit = insurer pays. Viatical = investor pays and owns the policy.
Watch Out
Key Person Premiums Are NOT Tax Deductible
Key person insurance premiums are NOT deductible by the business — even though the business is paying them. The IRS treats it as a capital expenditure, not a business expense. However, the death benefit received by the business IS generally income-tax free. This is the flip side worth knowing: no deduction going in, but tax-free benefit coming out.
