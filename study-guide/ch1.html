CHAPTER 1
Basic Principles of Life Insurance
Understand what insurance is, why it exists, the types of risk it covers, and the foundational concepts that underpin every life insurance policy.
Mutualization
Conversion of a stock company to a mutual company; policyholders become owners
Demutualization
Conversion of a mutual company to a stock company; policyholders receive stock or cash
Physical Hazard
A physical characteristic that increases the chance of loss (e.g., heart condition, smoking, hazardous occupation)
Moral Hazard
Dishonest tendencies that increase the chance of loss intentionally (e.g., fraud, arson, staging an accident)
Morale Hazard
Careless attitude toward loss because insurance exists (e.g., leaving keys in car, failing to lock home)
Risk Retention Group
Group of businesses in the same industry that pool liability risks; must be licensed in at least one state
Surplus Lines Insurance
Coverage placed with non-admitted (unlicensed) insurers when admitted carriers won't write the risk; NOT backed by guaranty fund
Key Terms
Insurance
A contract that transfers the risk of financial loss from an individual to an insurer in exchange for a premium.
Pure Risk
Risk involving only the chance of loss — no possibility of gain. Examples: death, fire, accident. Insurable.
Speculative Risk
Risk involving the chance of gain OR loss. Examples: gambling, stock market. Not insurable.
Insurable Interest
A financial stake in the continued life of another person. Must exist at the time of application.
Law of Large Numbers
The larger the group, the more accurately future losses can be predicted — foundation of insurance pricing.
Adverse Selection
The tendency of higher-risk individuals to seek insurance more than lower-risk individuals. Controlled by underwriting.
Indemnity
Restoring the insured to the same financial position before the loss — no profit. Life insurance is NOT a contract of indemnity.
Premium
The payment made to the insurer in exchange for coverage. Determined by age, health, gender, amount, and type of policy.
Core Concepts
Core Concept
The Purpose of Insurance
Insurance exists to transfer financial risk from an individual to a large group (the insurance company and all its policyholders). When you die unexpectedly, the financial burden doesn't fall on your family alone — it's shared across all policyholders who paid premiums. This is the pooling of risk.
Core Concept
Risk Management Methods
There are four ways to handle risk:
  • Avoidance — eliminate the activity entirely (don't drive)
  • Reduction — take steps to lower the risk (wear a seatbelt)
  • Retention — accept and self-fund the risk (self-insurance)
  • Transfer — shift the risk to another party (buy insurance)
Core Concept
Insurable Interest — The Rules
You automatically have insurable interest in your own life. You also have insurable interest in:
  • Your spouse, children, and dependents
  • Business partners (key person insurance)
  • Creditors (for the amount of the debt)
Key rule: Insurable interest must exist at the time of application — not at the time of the claim. A divorced spouse can still collect if they remain the named beneficiary.

Exam Tip
Insurable Interest Timing
The exam frequently tests WHEN insurable interest must exist. The answer is always: at the time the policy is applied for (application date), not at the time of claim. This is why a divorced spouse can still receive benefits if the policyholder never changed the beneficiary.
Exam Tip
Life Insurance Is NOT Indemnity
Property/casualty insurance IS indemnity (pay back actual value of loss). Life insurance is NOT indemnity — you cannot put an exact value on a human life, so the death benefit is whatever face amount was purchased. You can "profit" from life insurance proceeds.
Watch Out
Participating ≠ Mutual
A stock company can sell participating policies (ones that pay dividends). "Participating" refers to the type of policy, not the type of company. However, mutual companies always sell participating policies. Don't assume all dividend-paying policies come from mutual companies.
Mutualization & Demutualization
Core Concept
Stock ↔ Mutual Conversions
  • Mutualization = Stock company converts to Mutual; policyholders become owners and may receive dividends
  • Demutualization = Mutual company converts to Stock; policyholders receive cash or stock shares
  • Both conversions require state regulatory approval
  • After demutualization, policyholders are no longer owners — stockholders are
Hazards — High Exam Priority
Core Concept
Three Types of Hazard
  • Physical Hazard — measurable physical condition that increases risk (obesity, prior illness, dangerous occupation)
  • Moral Hazard — intentional dishonest act or tendency to commit fraud (arson, staging accidents)
  • Morale Hazard — careless attitude or indifference toward loss because insurance exists
Watch Out
Moral vs. Morale — The Classic Exam Trap
Moral = deliberate dishonesty (intentional). Morale = carelessness because "insurance will cover it." Example: "She left her car unlocked because it's insured" = MORALE hazard. The exam swaps these — read every scenario word for word.
Surplus Lines & Risk Retention Groups
Core Concept
Non-Admitted / Surplus Lines Insurance
  • Must try admitted carriers first — surplus lines is a last resort
  • A licensed surplus lines broker is required to place the coverage
  • NOT backed by the state guaranty association
  • Not subject to state rate and form approval (more pricing flexibility)
  • Used for unusual, high-risk, or specialty exposures admitted carriers refuse
Watch Out
Surplus Lines = No Guaranty Protection
If a surplus lines (non-admitted) insurer becomes insolvent, policyholders have no state guaranty association protection. This is one of the highest-tested distinctions between admitted and non-admitted carriers.
Core Concept
Risk Retention Group (RRG)
  • Businesses in the same industry pool their liability exposures
  • Must be licensed or chartered in at least one state
  • Members own the group
  • Exempt from some state insurance regulations under federal Liability Risk Retention Act
Quick Recall — Must Know Before Moving On
Pure risk = only loss possible = insurable. Speculative risk = gain or loss = NOT insurable.
Insurable interest is required at the time of application, NOT at the time of the claim.
Stock company = owned by stockholders = non-participating (generally).
Mutual company = owned by policyholders = participating (pays dividends to policyholders).
Law of large numbers = larger group = more predictable losses = more accurate premiums.
Adverse selection = sicker people buy more insurance = why underwriting exists.
Life insurance is NOT a contract of indemnity — no limit to what you can insure a life for.
4 ways to handle risk: Avoidance, Reduction, Retention, Transfer (insurance = transfer).
Moral hazard = intentional dishonesty. Morale hazard = careless attitude because insured. Exam swaps these — read carefully.
Surplus lines = non-admitted insurer; NOT covered by guaranty fund; admitted carriers must be tried first.
Mutualization = stock → mutual. Demutualization = mutual → stock. Both require regulatory approval.
Risk Retention Group = same-industry businesses pooling liability risk; must be licensed in at least one state.
